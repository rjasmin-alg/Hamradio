    DXing — Long-Distance Operating

    DXing is one of the most exciting disciplines in amateur radio — hunting distant and rare stations (DX stations) from around the world. "DX" comes from the telegraph abbreviation for "distance", and operators who pursue it are called DXers.

    The combination of propagation knowledge, strategy, patience, and operating skill makes DXing a pursuit that never loses its appeal — there is always a new country, a new island group, a new expedition to "catch".

    What Is DX?

    The definition depends on the band:

    • On HF (shortwave), DX typically means a contact with another continent — the Americas, Africa, Asia, or Oceania from Europe.
    • On VHF/UHF (2 m, 70 cm), even a 500–1,000 km contact via Sporadic-E propagation qualifies as DX.
    • Microwave (SHF) bands have their own DX horizons — sometimes only a few hundred kilometres due to atmospheric absorption.

    Why is DXing so popular?

    There are approximately 340 DXCC entities on the ARRL list. The entry-level DXCC award requires confirmed contacts with 100 different entities. "Mixed DXCC" with all 340 entities confirmed is achieved only by the most dedicated operators — and within the community it carries legendary status.

    DXCC Entities — What They Are and How They're Counted

    DXCC (DX Century Club) is the ARRL (American Radio Relay League) programme certifying contacts with distinct geographic entities. DXCC entities are not always sovereign states — the list uses its own rules based on geographic separation, political status, and distance. That is why it includes:

    Example Reason for separate entity
    Hawaii (KH6) Geographically remote, separate callsign prefix
    Alaska (KL7) Geographically separate from the continental USA
    Palagruža (9A/island group) Rare, small, remote from Croatian mainland
    Antarctica Special political status, extreme inaccessibility
    Ceuta and Melilla (EA9) Politically separate from mainland Spain

    Of the ~340 entities, around 40–50 are considered "rare DX" — their activations draw thousands of hunters worldwide.

    How to Work DX — Core Technique

    1. Listening Is Everything

    90% of DXing is listening. An experienced DX operator recognises a rare station by its signal, prefix, and operating style, and calls at the right moment. A beginner who immediately starts calling without hearing the exchange wastes precious time and annoys others.

    Listening routine: - Tune across the bands looking for unusual callsign prefixes - Monitor the DX cluster for fresh spots - Recognise the characteristic pile-up sound (a mass of stations calling in broken, overlapping rhythm)

    2. The DX Cluster — The DX Community's Central Radar

    The DX cluster is a network of servers where operators publish ("spot") active DX stations they have heard. A spot contains the frequency, DX callsign, mode, UTC time, and a comment.

    Popular resources: - DX Summit — the most widely used cluster web interface - DXMaps — visual map of spots in real time - DX Watch — alternative filtered display - HamAlert (mobile app) — push notifications for specific prefixes or bands

    Set filters on the cluster

    Unfiltered cluster displays are overwhelming. Filter by band (20 m, 40 m…) and region ("Europe only" or "missing DXCC only"), and you will immediately see only what is relevant to your operating session.

    3. The Pile-Up — When Everyone Calls at Once

    When a rare DX station is active — especially an expedition to an uninhabited island — hundreds or thousands of operators call simultaneously. This is called a pile-up.

    How to behave in a pile-up:

    • Transmit only your own callsign — once, clearly. Do not repeat it three times, do not add "CQ", "QRZ", or "please".
    • Listen to how the DX station responds — pick up the rhythm and the fragments of callsigns it is calling back.
    • Try briefly between others' calls, not while someone else is transmitting.
    • Be patient — it sometimes takes 15–30 minutes of active monitoring before you break through.

    Split operation — the most important rule

    Almost all rare DX stations work split — they transmit on one frequency (e.g. 14.195 MHz) and listen on another (e.g. 14.200–14.210 MHz, "UP 5 to 10").

    Never call on the DX station's transmit frequency! You will interfere with the DX station and anger the entire community. Always listen for where the DX says it is listening, then call there.

    QSL Cards and Awards

    Making the contact is only the first step. DXCC and other awards require QSL confirmations:

    • Paper QSL card — the traditional route, sent by post or through the QSL bureau
    • LoTW (Logbook of the World) — digital confirmation, the most widely accepted for DXCC. Requires an ARRL digital certificate.
    • eQSL — alternative digital confirmation, accepted by fewer awards
    • Club Log OQRS — direct QSL card from DX expeditions, paid via PayPal, fast and reliable

    QSL bureau — the economical route for paper cards

    Posting individual cards to the USA or Japan is expensive. The national QSL bureau (your local IARU member society) collects cards from your country's operators and forwards them in bulk to international bureaus, which then distribute them locally. Slow (6–12 months), but nearly free.

    DX Expeditions

    Some DXCC entities are so remote or inaccessible that they are only activated by specially organised DX expeditions (DXpeditions). Teams of 5–20 operators travel to an uninhabited island or restricted territory and operate for several weeks with multiple stations, 24 hours a day.

    Notable expeditions: - Bouvet Island (3Y0) — one of the most wanted entities in the world, a sub-Antarctic island in the Atlantic - Clipperton Island (FO/C) — French Pacific atoll, each expedition draws 100,000+ QSOs - Heard Island (VK0H) — sub-Antarctic Australian island, extremely rare - Palagruža (9A/island group) — our Croatian rarity, a small rocky island in the open Adriatic

    Follow DXpedition announcements at DX World and The Daily DX.

    DXing on Digital Modes

    FT8 and FT4 have revolutionised DXing — weak signals that could never be heard before now get through. Many DXers who never succeeded with SSB are now working new entities with FT8.

    At the same time, this has created a new dynamic: pile-ups on FT8 are different (automated sequence), and some DX expeditions operate exclusively on FT8, which is controversial among "purists".

    For setting up an FT8 station, see FT8 — Practical Guide.

    The DXCC Award — How to Start

    1. Register for LoTW — immediately. Without LoTW you will need paper QSL cards to document every entity.
    2. Club Log — use a logger that uploads to Club Log (Log4OM, N1MM+, DXKeeper). Club Log shows you which entities you are still missing.
    3. Monitor DX Summit — filter for "missing DXCC" and your preferred band.
    4. Start with accessible entities — Europe alone has over a hundred entities that are regularly active. When you reach 100, apply for DXCC Mixed.
    5. Progress toward the rare ones — with experience, better antennas, and propagation knowledge, tackle the more challenging entities.

    100 entities is achievable by anyone

    A dipole on 20 m and a standard 100 W transceiver are sufficient for the first 100 DXCC entities. The rest requires more patience, propagation awareness, and — sometimes — better antennas.
