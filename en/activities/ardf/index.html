    Amateur Radio Direction Finding (ARDF)

    Also known as "Fox Hunting", Amateur Radio Direction Finding (ARDF) is a technical sport in which competitors use a receiver and a directional antenna to locate hidden transmitters ("foxes") hidden in the field.

    The discipline combines physical activity (running or fast hiking through woodland), navigation skills, and an understanding of RF propagation. Croatia has one of the most successful traditions in this discipline at both the European and world level.

    How Does a Competition Work?

    Hidden automatic transmitters — "foxes" — take turns broadcasting short CW (Morse code) identification signals. In the standard discipline there are 5 foxes on the course, each transmitting in a cycle:

    • Fox 1: transmits for 1 minute (MOE in Morse)
    • Fox 2: transmits for 1 minute (MOI)
    • Fox 3: transmits for 1 minute (MOS)
    • Fox 4: transmits for 1 minute (MOH)
    • Fox 5: transmits for 1 minute (MO5)

    After each transmission there is a 1-minute pause while the next fox transmits. Competitors must find as many foxes as possible within the time limit (typically 60–90 minutes) and return to the finish.

    Why 'foxes'?

    The name comes from the early competitions in the Soviet Union in the 1960s where the sport was called "охота на лис" (hunting for foxes). The name has been used worldwide ever since.

    Categories and Bands

    ARDF competitions are held on two main frequency bands, each with different technical characteristics:

    80 m (3.5 MHz) — Classic discipline

    • Signal propagates as a ground wave — relatively predictable behaviour
    • Easier to locate by direction finding (longer wavelengths)
    • Equipment is somewhat simpler and more robust
    • Competitors use a ferrite rod antenna (flexible loop) for direction finding
    • Terrain is usually larger (forest, hills)
    • European and World Championships on this band are generally considered the more prestigious

    2 m (144 MHz) — More technically demanding

    • Shorter wavelengths mean more reflections from trees and terrain
    • Direction finding is harder due to multipath signals
    • Directional antenna is a 3–5 element Yagi or an H-antenna (special DF antenna)
    • Terrain is usually smaller (urban woodland, parks)
    • Reflections produce false bearings — experience and technique are critical

    Equipment

    For 80 m (3.5 MHz):

    Component Description
    Receiver Purpose-built ARDF receiver or modified AM receiver with good AGC
    Ferrite loop antenna Rotating loop on ferrite core — the null (minimum signal) direction is used for DF
    Attenuator Essential! Near the fox the signal is too strong — attenuate to achieve a usable null
    Compass To record and compare bearings from multiple measurement points

    For 2 m (144 MHz):

    Component Description
    Receiver FM receiver with a readable S-meter or a purpose-built 2 m ARDF receiver
    H-antenna or 3-el. Yagi Specially designed for ARDF — directional with good F/B ratio
    Attenuator Again essential — attenuate heavily close to the fox
    Side shields Metal shielding that blocks off-axis signals — for precise null determination

    Direction Finding Technique

    The basic technique is triangulation: 1. From one point, measure the bearing to the signal — record it on a map 2. Move 50–100 m in a direction that is not directly toward the fox 3. Take a new bearing 4. The intersection of the two lines = location of the fox

    More experienced competitors use the attenuation null method — they approach the fox until the signal disappears even at maximum attenuation (the null point).

    ARDF in Croatia

    Croatia has an exceptional tradition in ARDF. The Croatian national team has won multiple European and World medals, especially in the 80 m discipline.

    Organisation: - HRS (Croatian Amateur Radio Association) — organises Croatian ARDF championships - Regular club-level competitions throughout Croatia — Zagreb, Osijek, Rijeka, Split - European/World ARDF Championship — Croatia regularly participates and places

    Competition calendar: see HRS website and ARDF IARU.

    Getting Started With ARDF

    ARDF is ideal for amateur radio operators who want to combine technical knowledge with physical activity and navigation.

    Steps to get started: 1. Contact your local radio club — almost all have an ARDF section or members with knowledge 2. Attend an ARDF training session — clubs organise training where you can borrow equipment and learn technique 3. Participate in a club competition with borrowed or rented equipment 4. If you enjoy it — invest in your own ARDF receiver (€100–300)

    Purpose-built ARDF receivers are available from ham radio dealers and online. Popular brands include Radi-Scan, Elecraft, and various DIY projects available in the ARDF community.

    ARDF is especially good for young operators!

    ARDF is particularly popular with younger operators and youth. Combining running through woodland, solving the "puzzle" of where the signal is coming from, and applying amateur radio knowledge — all in one sport! HRS actively promotes ARDF through the YOTA programme.

    ARDF and Understanding Propagation

    ARDF is a practical school of propagation with concrete real-world examples:

    • 80 m ground wave and how it behaves in woodland vs. open terrain
    • 2 m reflections — why the signal "lies" to you when you are in a valley or near a metal fence
    • Antenna polarisation — why a horizontal antenna gives different results than vertical
    • Field antennas — improvising an antenna from available materials is a genuinely useful skill

    Many experienced ARDF operators have a deep practical understanding of RF behaviour in terrain — knowledge that transfers to every other branch of amateur radio.

    Other Fox Hunting Variations

    Beyond classic ARDF, several related activities exist:

    • Foxoring — combines orienteering with ARDF using micro-transmitters (milliwatt level), with many foxes scattered over a larger course area
    • ARDF Sprint — urban format, shorter rounds, city terrain
    • T-Hunt (Transmitter Hunt) — informal hidden transmitter hunt, popular in the USA, often done by car in suburban settings
