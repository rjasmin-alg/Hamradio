# Emergency Communications (EmComm)

Amateur radio operators are often the **last line of communication** when infrastructure fails — earthquakes, floods, violent storms, or cyberattacks can knock out mobile networks, internet, and telephone exchanges in minutes. In these situations an amateur radio operator with a battery, an antenna, and a transceiver becomes a vital link in the rescue chain.

EmComm (Emergency Communications) is not a separate branch of amateur radio, but rather the application of every skill you already have — knowledge of frequencies, propagation, operating procedures, digital modes — in the context of critical situations.

!!! warning "EmComm is not a game"
    Working in emergencies requires discipline, training, and coordination with competent authorities. Improvisation and unauthorised transmissions can complicate rescue operations. Operators wishing to do EmComm work should educate themselves and register with structured programmes (RMZO, ARES, RACES).

## Why Amateur Radio in Emergencies?

### Independence from infrastructure

Commercial systems (GSM, 4G/5G, internet) depend on:
- The electrical grid
- Fibre-optic cables
- Cell towers
- Server centres

All of this can disappear or become overloaded within the first hour of a disaster. An amateur radio station with a generator or solar battery continues to operate independently.

### Range without borders

- VHF/UHF repeaters (if they have backup power) cover locally and regionally
- HF shortwave signals cross continents without any infrastructure at all
- Satellite modes (on amateur satellites) operate independently of ground networks

### Verified operators

Licensed amateur radio operators are verified individuals who know the regulations, operating protocols, and frequency etiquette. This distinguishes them from unregulated CB users or improvised solutions.

## International EmComm Framework

### IARU EmComm

IARU coordinates the global EmComm network through:
- **Standardised EmComm frequencies** by region
- **IARU Emergency Telecommunications Guide**
- Coordination with international organisations (IFRC, UN-OCHA, WHO)

### ARES (Amateur Radio Emergency Service)

The largest voluntary EmComm programme, originally American (ARRL) but now global. Operators register with their local ARES group, undergo training, and make themselves available to local emergency services.

### RACES (Radio Amateur Civil Emergency Service)

US programme that integrates amateur radio operators directly into civil defence and government structures.

## EmComm in Practice — Historical Examples

| Disaster | Country | Role of amateur radio |
|---|---|---|
| Petrinja earthquake (2020) | Croatia | RMZO activation, coordination with DUZS |
| Hurricane Katrina (2005) | USA | ARES activation, only communications in parts of New Orleans |
| Turkish earthquakes (2023) | Türkiye | TRAC, coordination with UN agencies |
| COVID-19 coordination (2020) | EU | Winlink networks for medical supply logistics |
| Slavonia floods (2014) | Croatia | VHF/HF radio networks for evacuation coordination |

## How to Prepare

1. **Register with RMZO** — contact HRS or a local radio club
2. **Learn EmComm procedures** — see [Emergency Procedures](procedures.md)
3. **Set up a portable station** — a radio that runs on battery or solar, independent of mains power
4. **Learn Winlink** — [Winlink guide](winlink.md) for digital messages without internet
5. **Practise regularly** — EmComm skills need regular application (club drills, Field Day)
