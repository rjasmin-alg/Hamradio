# Introduction to CB Radio

CB (Citizens Band) radio is a public radio service open to everyone — no exam, no special licence. It sits between a walkie-talkie and amateur radio: far more capable than the former, but without the legal obligations of the latter.

In Croatia and across Europe, CB remains a living community — on motorways, in the hills, and among enthusiasts who enjoy simple, licence-free communication.

---

## A Brief History

CB radio has its roots in the United States in the 1940s, when the FCC (Federal Communications Commission) allocated part of the spectrum for non-commercial, public communication. The real boom came in the **1970s** — CB became a cultural phenomenon:

- American trucking culture built an entire language and identity around CB ("Smokey and the Bandit", "Convoy")
- "CB slang" entered popular culture — "breaker breaker", "10-4", "smokey" (police)
- In Europe, CB spread in the 1980s as a cheap alternative to telephones for drivers and small businesses

The digital revolution and mobile phones reduced CB traffic, but the community has not disappeared — it is still felt on motorways, hiking trails, and off-road tracks.

---

## Who Uses CB Today?

### 🚛 Truck Drivers and Transport Workers

Channel 19 (27.185 MHz) is the unwritten standard for communication on European motorways. Drivers exchange information about:
- Traffic jams and accidents
- Speed camera locations
- Road conditions (fog, ice, roadworks)
- Available truck stops and rest areas

### 🚙 Off-Road and 4×4 Community

CB is the standard in off-road convoys — more reliable than mobile phones in hilly or forested terrain where there is no signal, and convoy coordination is vital for safety.

### 🏕️ Hiking and Camping

Hiking and camping groups use CB for group coordination without call costs. Handheld CB radios are inexpensive and relatively robust.

### 🛡️ Preppers and Emergency Community

CB is popular in the prepper community as an independent communications network that does not depend on infrastructure (mobile network, internet). In the event of a power outage or disaster, CB remains operational.

### 📻 DX Enthusiasts

A smaller but dedicated group hunts long-distance contacts on 27 MHz via Sporadic-E and F2 propagation. This is CB DXing and differs very little from amateur radio DX.

## Differences from Amateur Radio

| | CB Radio | Amateur Radio |
|---|---|---|
| Exam | ❌ Not required | ✅ Technical exam |
| Licence | ❌ Not required | ✅ National licence |
| Power | up to 4 W (AM/FM) | up to 1,500 W |
| Frequencies | 40 fixed channels | entire bands (160 m – 70 cm and more) |
| Operating modes | AM, FM, SSB | AM, SSB, CW, FM, FT8, satellites… |
| Communication | Local/regional + occasional DX | Worldwide every day |
| Community | Drivers, off-road, preppers, hobbyists | Organised, with exams and clubs |
| Entry cost | €50–200 | €200–1,000+ (depending on goals) |

!!! tip "Path from CB to the amateur exam"
    If you are a CB user curious about more — the logical next step is the amateur radio exam. You already know the fundamentals: modulation, antennas, propagation. The technical knowledge you need is gained by studying for the exam — see [How to Become a Radio Amateur](../regulations/exams.md).

## CB Regulations in Croatia

CB radio in Croatia is regulated by **HAKOM** (Croatian Regulatory Authority for Network Industries). Key regulations:

- CB radio in the 26.960–27.410 MHz band falls under **general authorisation** — no special licence required for possession and use.
- Devices must carry a **CE mark** and be type-approved for the EU market (EN 300 433 standard).
- **Modifying power output** beyond the prescribed limits is prohibited — illegal amplification of CB radios ("turbo" and "linear" amplifiers) can result in fines and equipment confiscation.
- Channel 9 is reserved for emergencies — it must not be used for normal communication.

!!! warning "Illegal amplifiers (linear amplifiers)"
    Amplifiers that boost CB power from 4 W to 50–300 W are widespread in the CB community. However, they are:
    - **Illegal** — a criminal offence under the Electronic Communications Act
    - **Harmful** — such power interferes with adjacent channels, TV reception, and even medical devices
    - **Unnecessary** — on 27 MHz a better antenna always delivers more than extra power
    HAKOM carries out inspections and issues fines.

## CB Culture and Etiquette

Every radio community has its unwritten rules. CB is no exception:

- **Do not monopolise the channel** — exchange briefly, then free the frequency
- **Channel 9 for emergencies only** — do not use it for calling friends
- **Identify yourself** — though not a legal requirement as in amateur radio, it is courteous
- **No obscenities or offensive content** — CB is a public service accessible to everyone, including children
- **Listen before transmitting** — always check the frequency is clear

!!! note "CB \"10-codes\""
    The CB community uses **10-codes** — abbreviations for common phrases that speed up communication:
    
    | Code | Meaning |
    |---|---|
    | **10-4** | Received / Understood |
    | **10-20** | My location is… ("What's your 10-20?") |
    | **10-7** | Going off the air |
    | **10-8** | On the air, available |
    | **10-36** | What is the correct time? |
    | **10-33** | Emergency! |
